In this chapter, we're going to talk about the honest downside of loop engineering. We'll look at what goes wrong when a loop runs unattended, and how you can put limits around each failure before it ends up costing you. There are two risks we'll dig into. The first is runaway token cost, where an autonomous loop quietly burns through a budget. The second is slop, where weak verification lets a loop churn out a high volume of low-quality work. For each one, we'll tie the fixes back to the machinery you've already built, the guardrails and the evaluator from earlier chapters. And by the end, we'll make the case that none of this removes the need for engineering judgment. A loop relocates your judgment, it doesn't retire it.

Let's start with an honest accounting. The previous chapter made the case for loops. This one is the necessary counterweight. Everything that makes a loop powerful, the fact that it runs without you, iterates tirelessly, and supplies its own next prompt, is exactly what makes it dangerous when something is wrong. A person prompting turn by turn notices a mistake on the next turn and simply stops typing. A loop has no such instinct. Left to itself, it will repeat a mistake just as patiently as it repeats a success, spending real money the entire time.

So this chapter is deliberately not a sales pitch. Loop engineering is worth learning, but it isn't magic, and overselling it does you a disservice. The two risks we'll cover are the ones you'll actually run into in practice. Neither is a reason to avoid loops. Both are reasons to build them with the guardrails and the evaluator that earlier chapters described. And here's the genuinely good news. The same structure that makes a loop production-grade is also what makes these risks manageable. The fixes aren't new inventions. They're the components you've already met, pointed at the failure modes they were designed to prevent.

The first risk is the most immediate and the easiest to underestimate. An autonomous loop costs money on every iteration, and an unbounded loop can spend an unbounded amount. Each call to a generator agent consumes tokens, and a loop is, by definition, a machine for making many of those calls without asking permission in between. The very autonomy you built the loop for is what removes the natural brake, because there's no human reading each turn and deciding whether the next one is even worth it.

This goes wrong in a few characteristic ways. There's the stuck loop, which never reaches its goal but also never realizes it. It iterates, fails the evaluator, recycles the feedback, and tries again, forever, if nothing stops it. Each lap is cheap, but the bill is the sum of a great many cheap laps. Then there's the oscillating loop, which is a subtler version. The agent fixes problem A, which breaks B. The next iteration fixes B, which re-breaks A. The loop makes confident progress on every pass while actually converging on nothing, paying full price each time. And finally there's the expensive single attempt, because cost isn't only about how many times you iterate. One attempt that pulls an enormous amount of context into the prompt, or invokes an expensive model over and over, can blow a budget in a single lap. So capping iterations alone isn't enough.

The honest framing here is that this risk is structural, not occasional. It isn't a rare bug that only strikes a badly written loop. It's the default behavior of any loop that lacks explicit limits. A loop with no stopping condition doesn't usually finish. It has no concept of finishing at all.

So how do you mitigate runaway cost? The answer is the stopping condition and the guardrails you designed earlier, applied with discipline. The runaway risk is precisely what those guardrails exist to prevent, so the defense is to use them deliberately rather than as an afterthought. You set a hard iteration cap, a limit on the number of laps, which guarantees the loop terminates even if it never succeeds and never fails outright. That alone converts the stuck loop from an open-ended bill into a bounded one. You also set an independent cost cap, a dollar budget that stops the loop when cumulative spend crosses a threshold, regardless of iteration count. That's what catches the expensive single attempt that an iteration cap would miss. You really want both caps, because each one covers the other's blind spot. Beyond that, you make the success stopping condition real and checkable, because the cleanest way to stop spending is to actually finish. A goal the evaluator can objectively confirm, like tests passing or a rubric threshold being met, lets the loop exit the moment the work is done rather than iterating past completion. And when a budget is exhausted without acceptance, the loop should escalate to a human rather than fail silently or, worse, get restarted blindly. Detecting repeated no-progress, that oscillating loop, and escalating early saves the budget you'd otherwise spend just confirming the loop is stuck.

These limits live as data in the loop's configuration. In practice, that means you cap both the number of iterations and the dollars spent, you tell the loop to stop the moment its tests pass, and you wire up escalation so that when the budget runs out it notifies a human, and when it sees the same failure three times in a row it escalates early instead of grinding on.

There's a limitation worth being honest about. Tighter budgets trade away some of the autonomy that made the loop attractive in the first place. A cap set too low stops the loop before it can finish genuinely hard work. A cap set too high defeats its own purpose. There's no universally correct number. Choosing it is an act of engineering judgment, which is the theme this whole chapter builds toward.

The second risk is quieter and, in the long run, more corrosive. Slop is what a loop produces when it iterates quickly but verifies weakly. It's a high volume of output that looks like progress but doesn't hold up. Where runaway cost announces itself on an invoice, slop hides inside plausible-looking changes, passing review by sheer volume and confidence, until someone discovers that much of it is subtly wrong, redundant, or off-target.

Slop is the direct consequence of letting autonomy outrun verification. Remember the argument from the benefits chapter. Autonomy without an evaluator just produces mistakes faster. A loop is a multiplier. It amplifies whatever process you point it at. Point it at a strong verification process and it multiplies good work. Point it at a weak one and it multiplies slop just as efficiently. The danger is that the form of the output stays convincing even as the substance degrades.

This shows up in a few ways. There's confident-but-wrong output, where the generator agent reports success, the change reads cleanly, and nothing objective contradicts it, because nothing objective was ever consulted. The minimal loop that simply trusted the agent's own declaration that it was all done is the classic slop generator. There's volume mistaken for value, because a loop can generate far more code than a human would in the same time. Without a quality gate, that throughput becomes a liability. More surface area to review, more places for defects to hide, more plausible noise drowning out the signal. And there's what we might call Goodhart drift, where a loop optimizes against a weak proxy, a shallow test or a vague rubric, and learns to satisfy the proxy rather than the real intent. The metric turns green while the actual goal quietly recedes.

The mitigation for slop is the evaluator, and specifically the principle that the evaluator must be independent of the generator agent. Slop is what fills the vacuum when verification is weak, and a strong, independent evaluator is how you remove that vacuum. So you lead with strong, objective tests. An objective gate the agent can't talk its way past, a real test suite, a type checker, a build, a schema validation, is the backbone of slop resistance. It returns an unambiguous pass or fail that no amount of confident prose can fake. The stronger and more meaningful the tests, the less room there is for slop to slip through. Then you add a critic or rubric agent for what the tests miss. Objective checks confirm behavior, but they say little about readability, security, or whether the change actually matches intent. A second agent that scores the work against an explicit, versioned rubric covers that gap. You keep evaluation independent of generation, and this is the single most important property. The thing that judges the work must not be the thing that produced it, and it must not simply take the producer's word. Independent evaluation is what turns "the agent says it's done" into "an external check confirms it's done," which is exactly the difference between slop and verified progress. And finally, you make verification a hard gate, not a suggestion. You accept an attempt only when it passes the objective gate and clears the rubric threshold. A check that merely warns and lets unverified work through anyway isn't a defense against slop. It's slop with extra logging.

That acceptance rule is the same independent two-gate check from earlier, and it's worth seeing again as anti-slop machinery. In plain terms, the work is only accepted when two conditions both hold. First, the objective tests have to pass, an unfakeable pass or fail. Second, the independent critic's score has to clear an explicit threshold. Only when both are satisfied is the work promoted. Everything else is discarded. The agent cannot self-certify its way past either gate.

The honest limitation here is that an evaluator is only as good as the criteria you give it. A loop cannot verify quality you never specified. If your tests are shallow or your rubric is vague, the evaluator will faithfully wave slop through, and the loop will faithfully produce more of it. This isn't a flaw you can automate away by adding more agents. It's a demand on you, the human, to define what good means well enough that a machine can enforce it. And that brings us to the limitation that underlies both risks.

It would be dishonest to end a risks chapter by implying that the right configuration makes loops safe automatically. It doesn't. The deepest limitation of loop engineering is also the most easily overlooked. A loop does not remove the need for engineering judgment. It relocates it.

Every mitigation in this chapter ultimately rests on a human decision that no loop can make for you. There are the goals. A loop pursues the goal you give it, exactly as you phrased it. Deciding what is worth building, and stating it as a clear, checkable outcome, is human judgment. A loop will chase a badly chosen goal with the same tireless energy as a good one. There are the evaluation criteria. The evaluator enforces the standard you define, no more and no less. Deciding what to test, how strong the tests must be, what the rubric rewards, and where to set its threshold is human judgment. Slop, at root, is the symptom of evaluation criteria that were too weak to catch it. And there are the guardrails. The budgets, the iteration caps, the isolation boundaries, and the escalation path are all values you choose. How much money is this task worth? How many attempts before a human should take a look? What must never reach the main branch? Those are judgment calls a loop can't originate.

Seen this way, the two risks in this chapter are really the same lesson from two directions. Runaway cost is what happens when the guardrails embody too little judgment. Slop is what happens when the evaluation criteria embody too little judgment. In both cases the loop did precisely what it was told. The gap was in the telling.

This is why the shift from operator to architect is a relocation of judgment, not an elimination of it. As an operator you applied judgment a hundred times, one prompt at a time, and you could catch a problem mid-stream. As an architect you apply judgment fewer times but with far more leverage, and crucially, up front, before the loop runs unattended. The stakes on each decision rise precisely because you're no longer there to correct it turn by turn. A loop concentrates your judgment into a handful of high-leverage choices and then executes them faithfully and at scale. That's its power and its risk in a single sentence. It does exactly what you designed it to do, whether or not that's what you meant.

The practical posture that follows is neither hype nor fear. Build loops for the work that suits them, bound them with the guardrails and the evaluator this book has described, and treat the time you spend designing goals, criteria, and guardrails as the real engineering, because that's where your judgment now lives.

So let's recap what we've covered. Loop engineering is powerful, but it isn't magic, and an honest account names its risks plainly rather than overselling. The same structure that makes a loop production-grade is what makes its risks manageable, because the mitigations are components you've already met, pointed at the failures they prevent.

The first risk is runaway token cost. Because a loop makes many autonomous agent calls with no human brake in between, an unbounded loop can spend without limit, whether through a stuck loop, an oscillating one, or a single expensive attempt. That's the default behavior of any loop without explicit limits, not a rare bug. You mitigate cost with stopping conditions and guardrails. Set both a hard iteration cap and an independent cost cap, make the success condition real and checkable so the loop can actually finish, and escalate to a human on budget exhaustion or repeated no-progress instead of grinding on.

The second risk is slop. When iteration outruns verification, a loop produces a high volume of plausible-looking but low-quality output, confident-but-wrong changes, volume mistaken for value, and drift against weak proxies. A loop multiplies whatever process you point it at, good or bad. You mitigate slop with a strong, independent evaluator. Lead with objective tests the agent can't fake, add a critic or rubric agent for what the tests miss, keep the evaluator independent of the generator, and make verification a hard acceptance gate rather than a warning. And remember, an evaluator is only as good as the criteria you give it.

Above all, engineering judgment does not disappear. It relocates. A loop faithfully pursues the goals, enforces the evaluation criteria, and respects the guardrails you design. It can't originate any of those decisions. Runaway cost and slop are both symptoms of too little human judgment, in the guardrails and in the criteria. So the responsible posture is neither hype nor fear. Build loops for suitable work, bound them with guardrails and an evaluator, and treat designing goals, criteria, and guardrails as the real engineering, because that's where your judgment now lives, at higher stakes and greater leverage.
