In this chapter, you'll find out who this book is written for, the background knowledge it assumes you already have, and how it handles any concept that goes beyond that background. By the end, you'll be able to confirm that the material is pitched at your level before you invest your time in it.

So, let's start with who this book is for. This book is written for software engineers and technical practitioners who already use AI coding agents but are new to orchestrating them autonomously.

If you've ever opened an AI coding assistant, described a change, read its response, and then typed a follow-up prompt to steer it, then you are exactly the reader this book has in mind. You're comfortable being the human in the loop. You read each result, you decide what's wrong, and you prompt the next step by hand. What you haven't yet done is hand that supervising role over to a system that you designed.

That's the leap this book is built to support. It doesn't teach you what an AI coding agent is, or how to prompt one well. It assumes you can already do that. Instead, it takes you from operating an agent one turn at a time to engineering the loop that operates it for you. The center of gravity here is the orchestration system around the agent. That means discovering the work, running the agent in isolation, verifying the result, remembering progress, and deciding whether to continue or stop. It's not about the craft of writing a single good prompt.

There are a few readers who'll feel right at home here. The first is working engineers who lean on AI coding agents day to day and want to reclaim their attention from turn-by-turn babysitting. The second is tech leads and platform engineers who are evaluating whether autonomous agent workflows are worth adopting on their team, and who want an honest picture of both the benefits and the risks. And the third is tinkerers and early adopters who have run a quick agent loop in a shell script and want to understand how to evolve it into something dependable.

Now, if you've never used an AI coding agent at all, you can still read along. But a short period of hands-on experience with one will make every chapter land more firmly.

Let's talk about what you should already know. To keep the book focused on Loop Engineering itself, it assumes a modest amount of prior knowledge. None of it is advanced. All of it is the kind of background a practicing engineer typically already has.

First, version control, and Git in particular. You should be comfortable with the basics: commits, branches, and the idea that work can be isolated on a branch and merged back in later. Loops lean heavily on isolation, which means running each agent attempt somewhere separate, so that a bad result never contaminates your main code. The book uses Git concepts such as branches and worktrees to explain how that isolation is achieved. A worktree, by the way, is a Git feature that lets you check out more than one branch into separate working directories at the same time. The book reminds you of this wherever it matters, so a passing familiarity with branches is enough to get started.

Second, command-line usage. You should be able to read and run commands in a terminal. The worked examples begin with a simple shell loop and build up from there, so being able to follow a short bash script is assumed. That means variables, a loop, and invoking a command. You don't need to be a shell-scripting expert. You just need to be unintimidated by a terminal, and able to recognize what a handful of lines are doing.

Third, and most important, interacting with AI coding agents. You should have hands-on experience using at least one AI coding agent. For example, prompting it to write or modify code, and reading back what it produced. This is the most important prerequisite, because the whole book reframes that familiar experience. The thing you do by hand today is the thing a loop will do on your behalf. You need a felt sense of what an agent does well, where it goes wrong, and why a human currently has to stay in the loop, so that you can appreciate what it takes to remove that human.

There are also some things you do not need. You don't need prior experience building agent frameworks. You don't need knowledge of any specific orchestration tool or vendor. You don't need a machine-learning or model-training background. And you don't need any particular programming language beyond the ability to read the short, commented examples in the practical chapters.

Now let's look at how this book handles new concepts. Loop Engineering sits on top of several ideas. Some you'll know, and some you may not. This book follows one firm rule: any concept that goes beyond the prerequisites we just covered is defined before it's relied upon.

In practice, this works in three ways. First, terms are introduced before they're used. When a chapter needs a concept such as a Generator Agent, an Evaluator, a Harness, or a Stopping Condition, it explains the term in plain language at the point of first use, rather than assuming you already carry the definition. Second, acronyms are spelled out the first time they appear. For instance, the ReAct pattern, which stands for Reason plus Act, is named in full when it's first introduced in Chapter 4, before the book uses the short form. And third, a glossary backs the whole book. Every key term has a single, consistent definition collected in the glossary, so if you jump into a later chapter, you can always look a term up rather than hunting for where it was first explained.

The aim is that you never hit a wall of unexplained jargon. If you meet all three prerequisites, version control, the command line, and hands-on experience with an AI coding agent, then everything else the book needs, it will teach you along the way.

So let's recap the key takeaways. This book is for software engineers and technical practitioners who already use AI coding agents but have not yet built systems to orchestrate them autonomously. It assumes three prerequisites: familiarity with version control through Git, comfort with command-line usage, and hands-on experience interacting with AI coding agents. It does not assume prior experience building agent frameworks, knowledge of any specific tool or vendor, or a machine-learning background. And finally, any concept that goes beyond those prerequisites is defined before it's used, acronyms are spelled out at first use, and a glossary serves as the single source of truth for terminology.
