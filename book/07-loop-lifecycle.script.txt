In this chapter, we're going to see how the eight components from the last chapter come alive as an ordered, repeating lifecycle. This is the set of seven stages that a single unit of work passes through as a Loop runs. Work gets discovered or triggered. Agents are spawned in an isolated workspace. An agent acts to produce output. That output is evaluated. If it falls short, feedback is fed back and the work is tried again. State is saved. And then the Loop decides whether to continue, to spawn a sub-agent, or to stop. By the end of this chapter you'll understand the criteria behind that final three-way decision, and you'll be able to trace a single piece of work all the way from the moment it's triggered to the moment it terminates, and explain what happens, and why, at every stage along the way.

So let's move from parts to motion. In the last chapter we opened up the machine and named its eight parts. Now we set them turning. A list of components tells you what a Loop is made of. The lifecycle tells you how those parts interact over time. It tells you the order in which control passes from one part to the next, and what each one contributes on every trip around the circle.

The lifecycle is really the answer to a simple question. What actually happens, step by step, when a Loop runs? When you prompt an AI by hand, you supply that sequence yourself. You decide there's work to do. You ask the agent to do it. You read the result. You judge whether it's any good. You remember where you are. And you decide whether to ask again. A Loop takes exactly that sequence and encodes it as a repeating cycle, so it can run without you sitting in the chair. The stages we're about to walk through are those very same judgments, just made explicit and put in order.

A Loop moves work through seven ordered stages. Stages one through six make up the body of a single iteration, and the seventh is the decision that closes the iteration and chooses what happens next. Each stage is owned by one or more of the components from the previous chapter.

Stage one is discovering or triggering work. The cycle begins when work enters the Loop. Either the Loop discovers there's something to do, like the next unfinished item on a list, or an external event triggers it. This is the job of the Automation and Trigger component. It might be a scheduled job that fires overnight, a webhook on a newly filed issue, or a slash-loop style command. Nothing happens until this stage starts the iteration, and nothing here requires a human to type "go."

Stage two is spawning agents in an isolated workspace. With work in hand, the Loop creates a clean, sealed-off place for that work to happen, and it launches one or more agents into it. This stage draws on Worktrees and Isolation, a separate working copy of the code and a dedicated branch, so that whatever the agent does is contained and reversible. The agent that gets spawned is the Generator Agent, and as it starts, it's handed the project's Skills and Knowledge, the coding standards and the agent-guidance files, so that it works by your rules from its very first action.

Stage three is the agent acting to produce output. Inside its isolated workspace, the Generator Agent does the hands-on work. It reads the task, it edits the codebase, and it produces a set of changes. Where it needs to reach the outside world, like reading code from a source-control host or pulling details from an issue tracker, it does so through the Connectors. This is the only stage where new output is actually created. Every other stage exists to start it, to check it, to remember it, or to decide what to do about it.

Stage four is the Evaluator checking the output. The freshly produced output is now judged from the outside. The Evaluator, which acts as a critic or verifier, runs the test suite, reviews the changes, or scores the result against a rubric, and it returns a verdict. Is this good enough, or not? This is the stage that protects the Loop from confidently accepting wrong or low-quality work. And here's the crucial part. The judge is separate from the worker. The Generator Agent doesn't get to mark its own homework.

Stage five happens only when the output isn't good enough. In that case, the Loop doesn't throw the work away. It learns from the failure. The concrete signal of what went wrong, whether that's the failing test output, the reviewer's comments, or the rubric's low-scoring notes, is fed back to the Generator Agent as added context, and the cycle returns to stage three for another attempt. This feedback path is the heart of what makes a Loop a loop. Each rejected attempt makes the next one better informed, rather than repeating blindly. And this stage is conditional. When the output is already sufficient, the Loop skips straight ahead.

Stage six is persisting state. Whether the iteration fully succeeded or simply made partial progress, the Loop records what happened outside of any single agent's context, using the State and Memory component. It might update a to-do file, write notes to a scratchpad, or move a card on a project board. This is what lets the next iteration build on this one instead of starting from scratch. And it's also what lets you read the current state of the work at any time.

Stage seven is the decision. With the iteration complete and its state saved, the Loop reaches its decision point. Guided primarily by the Stopping Condition, it chooses one of three paths. It can continue to another iteration, it can spawn a sub-agent to handle a distinct piece of work, or it can stop. This three-way choice is important enough that it's worth looking at on its own.

If it helps, you can picture this whole sequence as a flow, with work coming in at the top, looping through the inner retry path whenever the output falls short, and arriving at the decision point at the bottom. But the steps we just walked through are the flow, so let's focus on that decision.

Every trip around the Loop ends at the same fork, and the path it takes is decided by clear criteria rather than guesswork. The Stopping Condition is the primary authority here, but the decision is genuinely three-way.

The first option is to stop. The Loop terminates when one of two kinds of rule fires. The first is success. The goal has been met. The Evaluator's tests pass, or the rubric score has reached its threshold. The work is done, so the Loop finishes deliberately and hands off its result, for example, by opening a pull request through a Connector. The second is safety. A guardrail has been hit. The maximum number of iterations is reached, or the token and cost budget is exhausted. Here the Loop stops without having succeeded, and it escalates to a human rather than spinning on. This is the rule that keeps a Loop from becoming a runaway process.

The second option is to continue. If neither a success rule nor a safety rule has fired, then there's still useful work to do and budget to do it with, so the Loop goes around again. In practice, continuing is the path taken after the feedback in stage five. The current task isn't finished, the attempt count and the budget are within bounds, so the Loop starts a fresh iteration on the same task with what it just learned.

The third option is to spawn a sub-agent. Sometimes the right move is neither to retry the same task nor to quit, but to delegate. When the work naturally splits into a distinct, self-contained piece, a focused sub-task that's large enough to deserve its own isolated workspace and its own evaluation, the Loop spawns a sub-agent to handle it. The sub-agent runs its own miniature lifecycle, with its own spawn, act, evaluate, and persist steps, and it reports its result back to the parent Loop. The criteria that favor spawning are decomposability, meaning the task cleanly divides, and independence, meaning the piece can be worked on without constant coordination. Choose this when a single agent grinding away linearly would be slower or messier than parcelling the work out.

So, in short. Stop when you're done or out of budget. Spawn a sub-agent when the work should be divided and delegated. And continue when there's more to do on the task at hand and the resources to keep going.

It's worth noticing that there are really two loops at work here. There's an inner loop, stages three through five, where a single task is retried with feedback until the Evaluator accepts it or a safety limit fires. And there's an outer loop, stages one through seven, that moves from one unit of work to the next. The decision at stage seven is where the Stopping Condition lives. It's the single place that chooses continue, spawn, or stop, and it's the reason the whole structure terminates instead of running forever.

Now, the lifecycle isn't some separate mechanism bolted onto the components. It is the components, observed in motion. Each stage is simply the moment when a particular component does its job. Stage one, discovering or triggering work, is the Automation and Trigger component. Stage two, spawning agents in isolation, brings together Worktrees and Isolation, the Generator Agent, and Skills and Knowledge. Stage three, the agent acting to produce output, is the Generator Agent working alongside the Connectors. Stage four, checking the output, is the Evaluator. Stage five, feeding feedback back and repeating, is the Evaluator handing its verdict back to the Generator Agent. Stage six, persisting state, is the State and Memory component. And stage seven, deciding whether to continue, spawn, or stop, is the Stopping Condition, with the Connectors on hand for the hand-off.

Trace that from top to bottom and you've followed a unit of work from the trigger that admitted it all the way to the decision that released it. It's the same journey the previous chapter previewed when it said the eight parts trace the path of a single unit of work through the Loop. The components are the nouns, and the lifecycle is the verb. With both in hand, you're ready for the next chapter, where you'll build a Loop that actually runs this cycle, starting from a bare-bones version and evolving it into a production-grade system.

So here are the key takeaways. A Loop runs as an ordered, repeating lifecycle of seven stages. First, discover or trigger work. Second, spawn agents in an isolated workspace. Third, the agent acts to produce output. Fourth, the Evaluator checks the output. Fifth, if it's insufficient, feed feedback back and repeat. Sixth, persist state. And seventh, decide whether to continue, spawn a sub-agent, or stop.

Stages three through five form an inner feedback loop. A rejected attempt isn't discarded. It's returned to the Generator Agent as added context, so each retry is better informed than the last.

The decision point at stage seven is three-way. The Loop stops on success, when the tests pass or the rubric threshold is met, or it stops on a safety limit, when the maximum iterations or the budget are exhausted, escalating to a human. It continues when the task is unfinished and resources remain. And it spawns a sub-agent when the work is decomposable and independent enough to delegate as its own mini-lifecycle.

The Stopping Condition is the single authority at the decision point. It's what guarantees the Loop terminates deliberately, rather than running forever.

And finally, each lifecycle stage is just one of the eight components doing its job. The components are the parts, and the lifecycle is those parts in motion.
